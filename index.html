Module One

The Anatomy of a Candlestick

Before candlesticks, we had line charts — dots connected by a line, one price point per interval (usually the close). A candlestick shows four times as much information at once. Once you learn to read the shape, the shape starts talking.

Every candlestick encodes four data points over a period of time: the open, the close, the high, and the low. The body is drawn between the open and the close. The thin lines extending above and below — the wicks (or shadows) — reach up to the high and down to the low.

Color is not decoration. A green body means the close was higher than the open — buyers won that interval. A red body means the opposite. So a candlestick is not just a price marker: it is a compressed narrative of what happened in that window.

Fig. 1.1 · AnatomyOHLC — Open, High, Low, Close
Green: buyers closed above where they opened. Red: sellers closed below the open. Wicks record how far price reached before it was rejected.

Body shape is a signal, not decoration

A long body with no wicks says something loud: price opened, moved decisively, and closed at or near the extreme. That is committed sentiment. A small body with long wicks says the opposite — buyers and sellers fought hard, and no one won.

You are learning to read a language. Once you know the alphabet, you stop second-guessing what the chart is saying. A stop sign doesn't require analysis; you see it and you stop. That's the level of fluency the rest of this course is aimed at.

Why this matters

Technical analysis is universal. Stocks, futures, forex, crypto — the same candles carry the same meaning on every chart. Learn it once, use it everywhere.

Module Two

Single-Candle Signals

Some single candles carry a reversal warning built into their shape. But context is everything: the same candle in the middle of chop means nothing. The same candle at an extended high can mean the trend is about to break.

Four candles do most of the work as reversal signals. Learn them by shape and by where they appear. A hammer at the bottom of a decline is powerful. A hammer sitting in the middle of sideways price action is noise.

Hammer
↑ Bullish reversal
Appears after a decline. Sellers pushed price down, buyers reclaimed it and closed near the top. Base being hammered out.
Shooting Star
↓ Bearish reversal
Appears after a rally. Buyers pushed up, sellers slammed it back down. The rejection is the message.
Doji
→ Indecision
Open ≈ close. Buyers and sellers finished the round tied. At extremes, a warning that momentum is stalling.
Hanging Man
↓ Bearish reversal
Same shape as a hammer, but at the top of a rally. Sellers finally showed up mid-session and price barely recovered.
The place I really get interested in the shape of a candle is when price is reaching really high levels — that's when reversal candles matter most. In the middle of chop, the same candle is nothing.
— On reading context

Two strategies, two mindsets

There are two ways to trade: with the trend, or against it. Most beginners are trend traders — it's easier to ride existing momentum than to predict a reversal. That's fine. But learning to recognize reversal candles keeps you from getting caught on the wrong side when the trend runs out of buyers.

Module Three

Multi-Candle Patterns

Individual candles talk. Groups of candles argue. When a cluster of candles forms a recognizable pattern, the signal is stronger than any single candle could be — because a pattern shows you the mechanics of buyer and seller behavior over time.

The Bull Flag

The most common trend-continuation pattern. A sharp move up — the flagpole — is followed by a shallow, orderly pullback — the flag. Buyers who missed the first move get a second chance; the trend resumes when price breaks the flag's high.

Fig. 3.1 · Bull FlagTrend continuation
A clean flag pulls back on light volume and does not retrace more than 50% of the flagpole. If the pullback goes deeper or lasts more than three or four candles, the pattern is broken.

Rules for a valid bull flag

  • The flagpole is real. Multiple long-body green candles with strong volume, not a slow drift up.
  • The pullback is shallow. Small red bodies. Anything more than 50% retracement invalidates the pattern.
  • The pullback is short. One to three candles is ideal. Four or more, and momentum is running out.
  • Volume drops in the flag. Selling should be tired, not urgent.
Trader's note

The first and second flags after a strong move are the cleanest. By the third and fourth pullback, the aggressive traders who set up the move have already taken profits. The late flags are where rookies get trapped.

The Flat-Top Breakout

Price hangs just under a clear horizontal resistance line — a flat top — and refuses to pull back. Every dip is bought inside a very tight range, often only ten or fifteen cents wide. When it finally breaks, the release is usually a single long-body green candle.

Fig. 3.2 · Flat-Top BreakoutCompression breakout
Price coils under a hard ceiling. Every candle tests the line. When one finally clears, the pent-up demand unloads in a single big candle.

The ABCD Pattern

A close cousin of the bull flag, but with a second, deeper leg. Price runs (A→B), pulls back (B→C), attempts a new high and fails, pulls back again to a higher low (D), then breaks out. The failed first attempt actually strengthens the setup — more buyers accumulated a second entry.

Fig. 3.3 · ABCDFailed flag → stronger breakout
The pattern is valid only if the second pullback (D) makes a higher low than the first (C) — the ascending support must hold.
Love what you're trading more than you love a perfect pattern. If a bull flag forms, trade the bull flag. If an ABCD forms, trade the ABCD. Trade the pattern that is presented.
— On not forcing setups

Module Four

The Four Indicators That Matter

Indicators are context. They don't replace candlesticks — they tell you where a candle is happening, who's in control, and whether the volume behind a move is real. Learn these four and skip the rest.

Indicator What it tells you Timeframe
VWAP Volume-Weighted Average Price. The true equilibrium of the session. Above it: bulls in control. Below it: bears in control. Also a well-respected level of support/resistance. Intraday
9 EMA Fast moving average. Price stair-steps back to this line during healthy uptrends — it's often where the cleanest pullback entries appear. Intraday
20 EMA Second line of defense. If the 9 breaks, price often finds support here. A common stop-loss reference on trend trades. Intraday
200 EMA The big-picture line. On the daily chart, above the 200 is bullish territory; below is bearish. Beaten-down names use it as their first major overhead resistance. Daily

Volume-Weighted Average Price

Of all four, VWAP is the most important intraday indicator. Every serious day trader uses it, which means the level itself becomes self-fulfilling: price behaves at VWAP because everyone is watching VWAP.

Fig. 4.1 · VWAP as support/resistanceBreak, retest, hold
This is a genuine tide change — not a wave inside a tide. When price breaks and holds VWAP, the character of the day flips.

Volume Bars

Volume is not an indicator you add. It's already there, at the bottom of every chart, telling you whether the move you're looking at is real. The rule is simple: strong moves need strong volume. A big green candle on light volume is a lie waiting to unwind.

The volume profile of a healthy bull flag

High-volume green candles on the flagpole. Light-volume red candles on the pullback. Volume comes back in on the breakout. If red candles have heavier volume than the green ones, the flag is failing — that's real selling, not a rest.

Skipping VWAP is like driving without looking at stop signs. You may not crash on any given day, but you're operating without the same information as every other driver on the road.

Module Five

Multi-Timeframe Alignment

The same setup can look clean on a 5-minute chart and broken on a 1-minute chart. When the timeframes agree, you have alignment. When they disagree, you have a warning.

Think of a five-minute candle as a container. Five one-minute candles fit inside it. That means every 5-minute setup can be zoomed in on to reveal the mechanics — and every 1-minute setup can be zoomed out to check whether it lives inside a healthy structure.

The 1-minute chart

The magnifying glass. Use it to time entries and check whether the current candle is confirming or fighting the higher timeframe. A high-volume red candle here inside an otherwise clean 5-minute flag is a caution flag.

The 5-minute chart

The working chart for most active traders. Setups form here: flags, breakouts, ABCDs. Entries confirmed here have more staying power than 1-minute noise.

The daily chart

The map. Before taking any intraday trade, look at the daily to find gaps, windows, and major moving averages. You are not looking to trade the daily — you're looking for the walls the intraday chart is going to run into.

What alignment feels like

You look at the 1-minute and say yes, I'd take it. You switch to the 5-minute and say yes, still take it. You check the daily and see clean overhead room. That's alignment. That's when you press the button.

The 5-minute chart says take it. The 1-minute chart says wait. Don't argue — trust the caution flag. The best trade of the day is the one where every timeframe is saying the same thing.
— On alignment

Module Six

Support, Resistance & the Space Between

Every price on the chart is either near a wall, sitting on one, or somewhere in the open. Learn to see the walls before they hit you.

Left and up

To map resistance on any chart, look left from the current price and then up. Wherever a prior candle made a high, price has a memory of it. That's a resistance level. Repeat leftward and upward — the highs of each prior candle become a staircase of levels.

Gaps and windows

Gaps are the empty space between one day's close and the next day's open. Windows are wider — a session that closed at one level and reopened at a very different one because something happened overnight. Both are areas with no memory of price. Once broken, there's often no resistance until the next real level. That's why breakouts through gaps move so fast.

Fig. 6.1 · GapEmpty space = no memory
Once price breaks the gap's lower edge, there is no meaningful resistance until the gap's upper edge. That empty space is where fast moves live.

Ascending and descending trendlines

Support and resistance are not always horizontal. Connect two or more successive higher lows and you have an ascending support line. Connect two or more lower highs and you have descending resistance. These slanted levels can hold for hours on an intraday chart or years on a daily chart.

How to draw them honestly

  • More touches = more validity. Two points define a line; three or more confirm it.
  • Wick tops vs. body tops. Try both. Keep the version that respects the most subsequent price action.
  • Adjust, don't force. If a line only works when you ignore three violations, it's not really the level.

Break, retest, flip

When a well-tested resistance is broken, the level often becomes support on the way back down — and vice versa. This is the most reliable trend-change signal on any timeframe. Look for it. When you see a break, retest, and hold, that's not noise. That's a regime change.

A trendline that connected in 2020 and holds again in 2023 is not a coincidence. These are the levels the whole market is watching. Not drawing them is trading blindfolded.
— On respecting the daily chart

Module Seven

Strategy in Practice

All the pattern recognition in the world is useless if you're applying it to the wrong instrument. Strategy is choosing where to point the toolkit before you use it.

Active traders make money from movement. Not opinions, not narratives — movement. So the first job every morning is not reading the news. The first job is finding what's actually moving.

A working checklist

  • Scan for movement first. Top gappers, top gainers, relative volume leaders. Whatever the market is putting on the table.
  • Confirm the data. Float, volume, catalyst. Is the move backed by real interest, or is it a low-float squeeze about to reverse?
  • Read the intraday chart. Long green bodies? Above VWAP? Clean volume profile? Or already extended off the top?
  • Zoom to the daily. Where is the 200 EMA? Where's the next gap or window? Is there overhead room to run?
  • Wait for a pattern. Bull flag, flat-top breakout, ABCD. Don't force. If the pattern isn't there, there's no trade.
  • Enter with a stop. Preferably at a technical level — below VWAP, below the 9 EMA, below the flag low. Never at a round number you picked out of nowhere.
The trap most traders fall into

You know the concepts. You know the patterns. You know where to get in and out. And then in the heat of the moment, you skip the checklist because fear or greed or fomo overrode your prefrontal cortex. That's not a knowledge problem. That's an emotional-regulation problem, and no amount of extra charting fixes it. Recognize which one you have.

What to trade

The specific instrument matters less than most people think. Candlesticks and technical analysis apply to any liquid market — equities, futures, forex, crypto. Trade what you enjoy watching, because you'll watch it more carefully. The right question is not what should I trade but what is moving today that I understand.

We don't make money if we're trading something that isn't moving. Volatility is the material we work with. No volatility, no trade.
— The active trader's first principle

Module Nine

Sessions & Liquidity Windows

The market is a 24-hour instrument only in name. In practice, it breathes in three phases — the Asian session, the London session, and the New York session. Where they overlap is where volatility lives. Where they don't overlap is where accounts get chopped up in thin, misleading price action.

Every market has a session structure. For equities, the session is bounded by the exchange's opening and closing bell. For forex, futures, and crypto, "session" really means which financial centers are awake and trading. Learning when to sit at the desk is as important as learning what to do when you're there.

Fig. 9.1 · The 24-hour clockTimes in UTC
The London/New York overlap (13:00–16:00 UTC) is the highest-liquidity window of the entire trading day. The Asia/London overlap is smaller but tradeable. Everything outside these bands is thinner.

The three sessions, characterized

Session Hours (UTC) Character Most active instruments
AsiaTokyo, Sydney, HK 00:00 – 09:00 Quieter. Tighter ranges. Trends established here often reverse in London. JPY pairs · AUD · NZD · Nikkei · HK stocks
LondonFinancial capital 08:00 – 16:00 The volume opens. Real trends form. Often sets the direction of the day. EUR · GBP · CHF · European indices · gold
New YorkUS markets 13:00 – 22:00 The heavyweight. Overlap with London is the most-traded window on earth. US equities · USD pairs · oil · US bonds

The US equity open is its own animal

For stock traders specifically, the US regular session runs 14:30 – 21:00 UTC (9:30 AM – 4:00 PM ET). The first thirty minutes after the bell is the single most volatile stretch of the day — the opening drive, sometimes called the opening range. Many day-trading strategies live and die on this half hour alone. The last hour (20:00–21:00 UTC / 3:00–4:00 PM ET), sometimes called "power hour," has a smaller but real volatility bump as institutions position for the close.

Pre-market and after-hours exist (11:00–14:30 UTC and 21:00–24:00 UTC) but volume is a fraction of regular hours, spreads are wider, and moves can look enormous on your chart while actually being tradeable by almost no one.

A rule of thumb

If you're day-trading currencies or equities and you're not sitting at the desk during either the London open or the London/NY overlap, you are probably fighting the market rather than trading with it. Trade the windows where the money is actually moving.

The candles look the same at 3 AM as they do at 3 PM. The volume behind them does not. Reading candles without reading the session is reading half the chart.
— On when to trade

Four dots means "when it works, it works cleanly." Three dots means "expect a lot of failed setups." No pattern in this table is above five out of five, because nothing in trading is above five out of five.

The four you already know

Bull Flag, Bear Flag, Flat-Top Breakout, and ABCD were covered in Module 03. The Bear Flag is a mirror of the Bull — same structure, opposite direction — and everything you learned about pullback depth, volume, and pattern integrity applies identically.

Six new patterns, in one paragraph each

Cup and Handle

Price forms a rounded base (the cup) followed by a shallow pullback (the handle) and then breaks the prior high. The rounded shape shows a gradual shift from selling to buying; the handle shakes out remaining weak holders before the real move. William O'Neil made this pattern famous in growth-stock trading; it works on daily charts almost exclusively.

Head and Shoulders (and Inverse)

Three peaks: a middle high flanked by two lower highs. The line connecting the two lows between the peaks is the neckline; a break of the neckline confirms the reversal. Inverse H&S is the same pattern flipped — three troughs, middle lowest, break of neckline signals the bottom. Reliability is medium because the pattern is often called too early — wait for the neckline break with volume.

Double Top / Double Bottom

Simplest reversal pattern in the book. Price hits a level, retreats, comes back, fails at the same level, and breaks down through the middle low. Or the reverse for a bottom. The reason it works is that the second failure at the level exhausts every remaining buyer (or seller), leaving only the other side to push price.

Ascending Triangle

A flat resistance line with a rising support line beneath it. Each pullback makes a higher low while sellers defend the same overhead level. Eventually the sellers run out and the flat top breaks upward. A descending triangle is the mirror image and signals a break to the downside.

Opening Range Breakout

Not a chart pattern in the classical sense, but a strategy. Define the high and low of the first 15 or 30 minutes of the session. When price breaks either extreme, take the trade in that direction with a stop on the other side. Simple, but requires discipline — the opening range often gets swept in both directions before finding its real direction.

Ten patterns is a survey, not a shopping list. Every serious trader I've ever met makes their living with two or three setups they know cold. The other seven are noise you learn to ignore.

Module Eleven · Critical

Responsible Account Management

Most blown-up accounts are not the result of bad chart reading. They are the result of bad sizing — traders who were right often enough to survive, but who lost more on their wrong trades than they made on their right ones. This module is the single most important one in the course. Read it twice.

The mathematics of ruin

Losses and gains are asymmetric. A 20% loss is not undone by a 20% gain — it requires a 25% gain. And the deeper the drawdown, the more punishing the arithmetic becomes.

Loss Remaining capital Gain needed to break even
5%95¢ on the dollar5.3%
10%90¢ on the dollar11.1%
20%80¢ on the dollar25%
30%70¢ on the dollar42.9%
40%60¢ on the dollar66.7%
50%50¢ on the dollar100%
75%25¢ on the dollar300%
90%10¢ on the dollar900%

This is why "just try to make it back" is not a plan. Below 50%, you don't make it back — you would have to double a shrunken account to arrive where you already were. Below 90%, the mountain is essentially unclimbable within a normal trading career. The lesson is not to fear losses; the lesson is to keep them small enough that arithmetic stays on your side.

The one-percent rule

The single most important rule in trading is this: never risk more than 1–2% of your account on any single trade. Not 1–2% of your position — 1–2% of your total account. If you have a $10,000 account, your maximum acceptable loss on a single trade is $100 to $200. Full stop.

This rule sounds conservative until you consider what it protects you from. At 1% risk per trade, you can be wrong ten times in a row and still have 90% of your capital. At 5% risk per trade, ten losses in a row leaves you with about 60%. At 10% per trade, ten losses leaves you with about 35% and needing a 185% return to recover. Compounding works in both directions.

Position sizing follows the stop, not the conviction

How many shares should you buy? The answer is never "as many as I can afford." The answer is derived from the stop:

The formula

Position size (shares) = (Account × Risk %) ÷ |Entry − Stop|

If your account is $10,000, your risk is 1%, your entry is $50, and your stop is $49 — you buy 100 shares. If the same setup has a stop at $45 — you buy 20 shares. Same conviction, same account, one-fifth the position. The stop dictates the size, not how much you like the trade.

R multiples: the trader's real unit of account

Professional traders don't think in dollars. They think in R — units of risk. If your stop is $1 away from your entry and you're sized to lose $100 on that trade, then $100 is 1R. A trade that makes $200 is a +2R winner. A trade that gets stopped out is a −1R loser.

Thinking in R does two useful things. It normalizes trades of different sizes so you can compare them. And it forces you to set targets in terms of your risk, not in terms of round numbers. A minimum acceptable target is 2R — meaning you're willing to risk one to make two. Below 2:1, the math turns against you fast: at 1:1, you need to be right more than half the time just to break even after costs.

The rules that keep accounts alive

  1. Never enter without a stopDecide where you are wrong before you enter. Type the stop into the platform the moment the entry fills. If your platform can't do it, get a new platform.
  2. Never move a stop away from your entryOnly toward profit. Moving a stop wider because "it'll come back" is how 1R losses become 5R losses.
  3. Cap risk at 1–2% per tradeNot per position, not per idea — per trade. Position size follows the stop.
  4. Cap daily loss at 3% of the accountThree full-risk losers is your day. Close the platform and walk. Tomorrow is another day.
  5. Take at least 2R on your winnersIf you can't make twice what you risk, the trade isn't worth taking.
  6. Journal every tradeEntry, exit, size, thesis, and outcome. You cannot improve what you don't measure. Review weekly.
  7. Cut losers fast, let winners breatheThe reverse — cutting winners fast and hoping losers come back — is the single most common losing pattern.
  8. Never revenge-tradeTrying to "make it back" immediately after a loss is how a bad hour becomes a bad week.
You do not need to be right most of the time. You need to make more when you're right than you lose when you're wrong. Everything in this module is designed to enforce that single arithmetic truth.
— The only rule that matters

Module Twelve

Brokerages Compared

Where you open your account determines what rules you play under. Regulation, leverage, investor protection, tax treatment — all of it varies dramatically across borders. This module compares the structural differences, not specific brokers. Brands change; the framework doesn't.

Jurisdiction Regulator Protection Max leverage Day-trading rule Tax reporting

United KingdomLSE-listed brokers FCA FSCS — £85k 30:1 major FX · 5:1 stocks / CFDs None Self-report (CGT)

The Pattern Day Trader rule (US)

The single most consequential US regulation for small accounts. If you place four or more day trades (round trips) within a rolling five-business-day window in a margin account, you are classified as a Pattern Day Trader. Once flagged, you must maintain at least $25,000 in equity at all times, or the broker restricts you from further day trades until you fund the account back above the threshold.

There are only three real workarounds: (1) hold the position overnight — swing trading is not affected; (2) use a cash account, which limits you to trading with settled funds (T+1 or T+2 settlement — slow but no PDT); (3) trade futures or forex, which are regulated separately and have no PDT rule.

Leverage: freedom or rope

Overseas brokers routinely offer 100:1 or higher leverage on forex. In the US, retail forex is capped at 50:1 on majors. This is not a coincidence — the leverage cap exists because retail traders using 500:1 leverage overwhelmingly lose their entire deposit within months. High leverage is not a feature you take advantage of; it is a feature that takes advantage of you.

The rule of thumb: use only enough leverage to bring your position size to the level your risk rules already allow. If a 1% risk of your account is $100, you don't need 500:1 leverage to get there. You need discipline, not leverage.

What about prop firms?

Proprietary trading firms — Topstep, FTMO, Apex, and similar — offer an alternative structure entirely. You pay for an evaluation, demonstrate you can trade profitably within their risk parameters, and are then "funded" with the firm's capital (or a simulated version of it). Profits are split, typically 70/30 to 90/10 in favor of the trader.

The upside: no PDT restriction (because you're not trading your own capital), leverage sized to the account, and no personal risk beyond the evaluation fee. The downside: strict daily loss limits, maximum drawdown rules that will drop you the moment you breach them, and evaluation fees that add up if you fail the challenge repeatedly. Real for some, expensive tuition for many.

A note for US persons

If you are a US taxpayer, opening an account with an overseas broker does not exempt you from US tax reporting. FATCA, FBAR, and PFIC rules apply. Consult an accountant familiar with cross-border trading before opening an overseas account — the reporting burden is real, and penalties for missing it are severe.

Regulation is not a cage. It is the ceiling that stops a bad year from becoming a life-changing one. The overseas broker offering you 500:1 leverage is not doing you a favor.
— On where to open the account

This module compares regulatory structures, not specific companies. Broker offerings change constantly. Before you open any account, verify current terms directly with the broker and confirm they are authorized in your jurisdiction with your local regulator.
